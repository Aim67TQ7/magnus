# Plate Magnet Technical Reference

## Overview

Plate magnets are mounted above conveyors, chutes, or hoppers to capture tramp ferrous metal from material streams. They are the simplest magnetic separator — no moving parts, no power, passive protection. Available in permanent (ceramic and rare earth) configurations.

## Engineering Design Framework

### Magnet Selection Criteria
- **Neodymium-27**: Optimal strength-to-cost ratio for most applications
- **Flux density**: 0.463 T for 1/2x2x2 blocks, 0.317 T for 1/2x1x2 blocks
- **Construction**: 304 stainless steel housing with variable mild steel backing

### Performance Testing
- **Test plate**: 1/4" x 1" x 4" steel standard
- **Protocol**: Three readings per position, averaged
- **Air gap sensitivity**: Linear degradation with distance
- **Pull force**: Chatillion force gauge validation

## Application Guidelines

### When to Use Plate Magnets
- Conveyor belt applications where material passes underneath
- Chute/gravity flow where material slides past face
- Simple tramp protection (bolts, nuts, wire)
- Low to moderate contamination frequency
- Manual cleaning is acceptable

### When NOT to Use Plate Magnets
- Deep burden depths (>6") — magnet cannot reach bottom of material stream
- High contamination rates requiring frequent cleaning — use self-cleaning overband
- Fine particle capture — use grate/drawer magnets for powders
- Non-ferrous detection required — use metal detector

### Installation Best Practices
- Mount as close to material stream as possible (minimize air gap)
- Material stream should be spread thin for maximum exposure
- Clean on regular schedule before saturation reduces efficiency
- Support structure must be non-ferrous (300-series stainless) within 18-24" of magnet

## Sizing Rules

- Plate width should cover full belt/chute width
- Effective reach determines maximum burden depth that can be protected
- For burden depths exceeding plate reach, upgrade to suspended overband or electromagnet

## Deploy Neodymium When
- Weight is operationally critical
- Space constraints limit ceramic options
- Automation integration is planned
- Safety regulations favor lighter equipment

## Deploy Ceramic When
- Initial cost optimization is primary driver
- Traditional installations with adequate infrastructure
- High-temperature environments (>176°F)
- Proven application with established ROI
